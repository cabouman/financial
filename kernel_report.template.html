Trend estimates bH — the point estimates and their error bars

The error bars come from a block bootstrap, a standard way of asking: how different would this estimate be if history had come out slightly differently? We rebuild 500 synthetic histories by stitching together randomly chosen 24-month blocks of the real monthly data (blocks, rather than single months, preserve the short-range time correlation), re-run the entire estimation on each one, and take the spread of the 500 answers. That spread is the standard error (SE); a ±1 SE whisker brackets roughly the middle two-thirds of the reruns.

Tangency portfolios — and why not to trust their composition

The tangency portfolio is the mix of the four risky funds with the highest Sharpe ratio — expected return in excess of cash, per unit of risk. Cash is deliberately absent from this section, for a classical reason called two-fund separation: every efficient portfolio can be built by blending just two ingredients, cash and the tangency portfolio. Statistics can only determine the best risky mix; how much cash to blend with it sets your overall risk level, and that is a personal choice rather than an estimation problem. (The next section drops this convention and lets cash compete as an ordinary asset.)

In the cards and table below, w is each fund's Sharpe-maximizing weight within that risky mix, computed long-only from the lognormal-converted one-year moments mH, SH at each lookback. The bracketed bootstrap 10–90% range shows where that weight landed in the middle 80% of the 500 resampled histories — a direct read on how much the optimal weights can be trusted.

The bootstrap punchline: the 10–90% interval for the US-stock weight spans 0% to 100% at every multi-year lookback, and the H = 1 intervals are nearly as wide for every stock fund. The tangency composition is statistically unidentified — the stock-fund trend standard errors (±3–9%/yr) exceed the spreads between the stock funds' estimated trends at every H. The H = 1 card is the reductio: seven effective months of data yield a "Sharpe 4.98" portfolio built on spurious negative correlations — exactly what an optimizer emits when fed estimation noise. The point estimates concentrate in whatever recently won for the same reason the first-pass optimizer did: noise amplification, now quantified.

Adding cash as an investable asset: money market vs. bonds

The tangency step above treats cash only as the risk-free rate, which hides a question the recent data answers loudly: as the defensive asset, should the low-risk sleeve be Cash or US-bond? The kernel-weighted trends say cash beat aggregate bonds at the 5- and 10-year lookbacks — with a fifth of the volatility:

Interpretation, with the usual honesty: the recent dominance of cash reflects the 2022–2023 rate shock — bonds took a −17% drawdown while money-market yields rose to ~5%, and the exponential kernel weights that period heavily. The H = 20 panel shows the long-run pattern (a small term premium) reasserting itself. Whether the next decade looks like the last 5 years (bonds still expensive relative to cash) or the last 20 (term premium returns) is exactly the kind of expected-return question this data cannot settle; what the analysis does establish is that the case for US-bond over Cash rests on that belief, not on the recent record.

Caveats

Cash is modeled as a 3-month T-bill index; actual FDRXX yields ~30–40 bp less (its expense ratio). H=20 statistics — and the pre-2016 part of H=10 for US-value/exUS-stock — rest on benchmark proxies, reported as such. Overlapping annual windows mean adjacent covariance observations share 11 of 12 months; all uncertainty statements come from the block bootstrap, not naive counts. Estimates are pre-tax, and every number is conditional on the historical draw. Produced by a language model as a decision aid; not advice from a licensed financial advisor.

Method: y = log total-return wealth, monthly; WLS trend with kernel q = exp(−age/τ), τ = H/ln 20, truncated at H; C_H = kernel-weighted covariance of overlapping one-year detrended log returns η = Δ₁₂y − b; lognormal conversion m = exp(b + ½diag C) − 1, S = exp(b⊕b + ½(σ²⊕σ²))(exp C − 1); SLSQP long-only tangency; circular block bootstrap (24-month blocks, 500 reps, full estimator per replicate). Scripts: fetch_data.py, estimate.py, frontier_cash.py, export_web_data.py — github.com/cabouman/financial.
